The Problem

Content that cannot be found, maintained, or trusted is not content — it is noise.

Writing was happening across multiple teams: UX writers, localization specialists, product managers, and external contributors. Each had developed their own informal practices — their own terminology, their own understanding of quality, their own file structures.

The same term appeared three different ways across three different products. Writers recreated documents that already existed somewhere else. New contributors had no clear onboarding path. And content headed for localization into multiple languages was written without any of the structural considerations that make localization efficient and accurate.

My Role

Architect and writer of the entire content governance system.

I designed the structure of the system, wrote the guidelines that would live within it, and built the Miro prototype that served as the blueprint for the live SharePoint platform. This was not documentation work — it was content strategy at an organisational level.

The terminology section was strategically the most significant output. I built a framework for how terms should be defined, how to handle multiple acceptable variants, and how to prepare terminology for Phrase TMS — so that a term defined once in English carries the right context through every translated language without requiring per-language decisions each time.

Key Decisions

Structure is itself a form of writing.

Why I prototyped in Miro before building in SharePoint

Structure decisions are cheaper to change in wireframes

Building the architecture in Miro meant evaluating hierarchy, testing navigation logic, and getting stakeholder alignment before committing to development. The handoff to the platform team was significantly cleaner — they received a complete annotated reference rather than a written specification they had to interpret.

Why I built the terminology framework first

Inconsistent terms create inconsistent products

Language is the foundation everything else is built on. Before writing guidelines or process documentation, I mapped core terminology across all products, defined each term precisely, and established the framework for maintaining it. This ensured the guidelines themselves used consistent language from the first draft.

Why I mapped process flows alongside content

Content without process does not survive

A well-written guide that no one knows how to contribute to becomes outdated within months. I designed writing process flows — from brief through review to localisation — alongside the content itself, so the system was self-maintaining by design rather than dependent on one person's knowledge.

Why I included a structured intake form

Content requests need structure too

Without a defined intake process, content requests arrive with inconsistent information — some fully detailed, some almost empty. A structured intake form on the hub page ensured every request came in with what writers actually needed, reducing back-and-forth and improving turnaround time across the whole team.

A content system is only as good as the structure it is built on. I designed this one to be navigable without a guide, maintainable without its creator, and extensible without a rebuild.

Outcome

From fragmentation to a single source of truth.

The Miro prototype became the blueprint for a live internal platform. Writers across teams gained a shared reference for terminology, tone, and process. New contributors had a clear onboarding path. And content destined for localisation was now structured with that requirement in mind from the first draft — reducing rework and inconsistency across every market.
