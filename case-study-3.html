Improving Error Communication
in a Hearing Aid App

Users were failing to connect their hearing aids due to unclear system feedback. The error messages did not indicate whether the system had failed or was still searching — leading to confusion, repeated attempts, and avoidable support calls. I redesigned the error communication to give users clear system states, specific guidance, and a tone that felt calm rather than alarming.

The Problem

The app knew something had gone wrong. It just could not communicate it clearly.

During the Bluetooth pairing flow, users reaching an error state had two critical questions left unanswered: has the system stopped searching, or is it still trying? And what should I do right now?

Without clear answers, users defaulted to the only action available — pressing Restart and waiting — sometimes repeatedly. Others gave up and called support for a problem that the interface should have resolved with better copy. The annotation visible in the Figma file at the time captures the problem exactly: "Not sure if the background screen is when discovery is complete or it is still searching for HAs."

Before and After

Two different failures — two distinct messages, each matched to the actual situation.

The problem with what existed

  • Ambiguous title gave no indication of whether the error was final or ongoing
  • Body copy described the timeout technically without saying what to do
  • No distinction between discovery timeout and total connection failure
  • Single Restart CTA with no explanation of why restarting would help
  • Tone created anxiety without giving any sense of recovery path

What I designed instead

  • Two distinct error variants: discovery timeout and no hearing aids detected
  • Title names the system state precisely: "Connectivity error" vs "Hearing aid connection failed"
  • Body copy explains what happened, why, and what the user should do
  • Specific actionable instruction: check Bluetooth, restart the process
  • Calm, direct, recoverable tone consistent across both states

The Work

Two screens, two problems, two carefully different messages.

The screens below show both redesigned error states. The left handles a discovery timeout — hearing aids were present but the process did not complete within the expected time. The right handles a connection failure — no hearing aids were detected at all. These are different user situations and required different copy, even though they might look visually similar at a glance.

The left state tells the user their hearing aids are disconnected and asks them to restart to complete the pairing. The right state explains that Bluetooth is required, no devices were found, and instructs the user to check their phone settings before restarting. Both are specific. Neither is generic.

The Framework

Every error message I write answers three questions.

Question 1

What happened?

The title names the system state precisely. "Hearing aid connection failed" tells the user the attempt is over — not still in progress. This single change eliminates the ambiguity that caused most repeated restart attempts, because the user now knows where they stand.

Question 2

Why did it happen?

The body copy explains the cause in plain language — Bluetooth requirement, device proximity, discovery timeout — without requiring the user to understand the technical process. Just enough context to make the next action feel logical rather than arbitrary.

Question 3

What should I do?

Every error state ends with a specific, actionable instruction. Not "Try again" but "Restart them to complete the process" or "To continue operating through your phone restart the process." The specificity signals to the user that the instruction is deliberate, not a placeholder.

The Tone Decision

Calm, not apologetic

Error messages that over-apologise create anxiety without adding information. The redesigned copy is matter-of-fact — acknowledging the problem without amplifying frustration. For a hearing aid user who may already feel uncertain about technology, that tonal difference matters more than most product teams realise.

Error messages are the moments where users need the most clarity and often receive the least. Getting them right is not a nice-to-have — it is the difference between a user who recovers and one who gives up.

Outcome

A reusable pattern, not just two fixed screens.

The redesigned error states gave users the information needed to take the right action on the first attempt. Beyond these two screens, the three-question framework — What happened, Why it happened, What to do — became a reusable content pattern applied across other error states in the product, creating a more consistent voice and more predictable user experience throughout the app.
